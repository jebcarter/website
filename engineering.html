Hero's Heat Engine

I completed this project as a part of the course MAE 221, Thermodynamics, with a small group of classmates at Princeton University.

Background

This project was an open-ended assignment given to engineering students as the final project of the laboratory section of our thermodynamics class. The objective of the assignment was to design and build a real-world application of the principles of thermodynamics and to collect data on that realization, evaluate its value (or lack thereof) from a thermodynamic point of view, and write a report on our idea, methodology, and findings.

Design

We decided to build a heat engine, and to analyze its power output, efficiency, and practicality. Because of our limited budget and time constraints, we elected for the Hero's Heat Engine, one of the oldest and simplest engines in modern engineering, as our design. An illustration of Hero's engine as it was originally designed in the 1st Century A.D. is shown (right). Hero's engine is a steam-powered engine that converts heat to rotational motion by heating water inside a central orb and allowing the pressurized vapor to escape through small nozzles away from the orb. The function of the nozzles are to accelerate the vapor and direct its outflow perpendicular to the axis of rotation, thus producing a thrust that spins the whole engine.

The design of our engine (right) was very similar to the original design. The core of the heat engine was a cast iron 2-inch pipe with a length of 6 inches. Both the bottom and top of the pipe were sealed with a similar cast iron cap. On the sides of the engine core, two arms extended outward, which served as the nozzles for the steam to be directed in a perpendicularly to induce rotational movement. The steam engine core had a small opening on the top to allow for water injection during the experiment, and the engine itself was mounted on a low friction ball bearing, which allowed for controlled rotational movement.

Testing

Our testing phase included a series of motion tests and motion tests. During motion testing, the engine apparatus was suspended over a stairwell. Coiled around the central axis of the heat engine was a string, which, through a low-friction pulley, connected the engine to a mass at the bottom of the stairwell. During successive trials, masses of 5g, 10g, 15 g, and 20g were attached to the string at the bottom of the stairwell. A blowtorch was used to heat the engine, which spun up and lifted the mass up the stairwell. During each trial, we measured the time required to lift the mass, the angular velocity of the engine, the fuel consuption of the blowtorch, and the mass water loss.

After all of the motion tests were completed, the group ran two “stationary” tests, during which the device was fixed in place. In the first test, the saturation temperature and pressure within the device were measured as the water heated and transformed into steam. This data was used to calculate theoretical efficiencies of the device. The second stationary test measured the change in mass of water during the time period before the engine began to move. This test was important because it established how much of the mass was used to do work and how much was wasted in heat-up.

Conclusion

We used several techniques to calculate the heat input, heat input rate, work output, power output, and efficiency. Heat input and heat input rate were calculated using the water mass consumption and the heat capacity of water. Power output was calculated as the torque multiplied by the angular velocity. Work output, on the other hand, was calculated as the force applied to lift the mass times the distance. Finally, efficiency was calculated twice, once as the ratio of power output to heat input rate and again as the ratio of work output to heat input. We also calculated theoretical values for heat input, power output, and efficiency using thermodynamic principles and the parameters of our apparatus.

As expected, the efficiency of our engine was extremely low (on the order of 10-3). This was not all that surprising, since Hero's engine is a very simple design, but it was an exciting proof-of-concept for the principles of thermodynamics and a great exploration of those concepts in real life.
